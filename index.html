Security and Surveillance

Emotion recognition is being used in security to detect aggressive or suspicious behavior. For example, if a person exhibits signs of anger or fear, it may trigger a security response to prevent potential violence or disturbances. Some security systems in airports and high-risk locations use emotion recognition to detect stress, aggression, or fear , which can help prevent security breaches

Advertising, Marketing and Retail

Used to enhance campaigns and improve consumer engagement by analysing customer reactions to tailor content more effectively.
Coca-Cola has used emotion recognition to track consumer reactions to its ads and ensure that its content triggers positive emotions like happiness and excitement. Their "Share a Coke" campaign, for example, relied on emotional engagement to build stronger consumer connections.

Healthcare

Emotion recognition can play a vital role in mental health diagnostics and therapy by tracking patients' emotional responses to different stimuli or therapy sessions. This can provide valuable insights into a person's psychological state. Research shows that emotion recognition tools can help detect depression and anxiety disorders early by analyzing subtle emotional cues in facial expressions and voice patterns.

Emotion recognition systems are used in autism therapy to help individuals on the autism spectrum understand and interpret emotions, improving social interactions. Emotion recognition programs like EMO have been used in clinical settings to aid children with ASD by teaching them how to recognize emotional expressions in others.

Education

Emotion recognition can assess students' emotional states to help educators understand their engagement levels during classes. For instance, students may display signs of frustration or boredom when facing a challenging task, and emotion recognition can help address these issues in real-time.

Kahoot! an educational platform, uses emotion recognition to assess how students feel during lessons and adapt content to enhance engagement.

Automotive Industry

Emotion recognition is used in advanced driver-assistance systems (ADAS) to monitor a driver's emotional state. This can help detect signs of drowsiness, road rage, or stress, and activate safety measures such as alerting the driver or taking over certain driving functions. Companies like Mercedes-Benz and Nissan are developing systems that use emotion recognition to improve road safety by detecting emotional distress and drowsiness

Problems with Bias

Many emotion recognition models are trained on datasets that lack diversity , often overrepresenting certain racial, gender, and age groups. A model trained on Western facial expressions may misinterpret Asian or African facial expressions due to cultural differences in how emotions are displayed.

Emotion recognition systems are often biased against neurodivergent individuals, including those with autism, ADHD, anxiety disorders, and other neurological differences. These systems rely on standardized facial expressions and body language that may not accurately reflect the emotional expressions of neurodivergent people, leading to misclassification, discrimination, and unfair treatment.

Emotion detection models rely on predefined emotion categories (e.g., happiness, sadness, anger). However, emotions are complex and context-dependent. Misidentifying neutral expressions as anger could lead to unjust targeting in surveillance or law enforcement.

Real-World Consequences of Bias:

Workplace Surveillance: Emotion tracking in workplaces could unfairly label employees as "unproductive" or "unengaged," leading to job loss or discrimination. Additionally, AI emotion tracking in job interviews may reject neurodivergent candidates for having "low engagement" or "negative emotional tone."

Law Enforcement: Police surveillance systems using biased emotion detection might disproportionately target minorities.
Emotion AI used in policing and airport security may misinterpret atypical expressions as deception or nervousness. An autistic traveler showing anxiety at a security checkpoint could be wrongly flagged as suspicious.

Education: Schools using emotion detection AI to monitor student engagement may misclassify neurodivergent students as disengaged or inattentive

Mental Health Misdiagnosis Healthcare AI using biased emotion recognition could misinterpret distress, leading to incorrect psychiatric evaluations. AI-powered mental health tools may fail to recognize emotions in neurodivergent users, leading to incorrect assessments and treatment recommendations.

How to Reduce Bias in Emotion Recognition Surveillance:

Diverse Datasets: Use training data that includes a wide range of ethnicities, genders, and cultural backgrounds. Include neurodivergent expressions and behaviors in training datasets

Recognize Non-Traditional Emotion Cues: AI should learn that emotion is expressed differently in different neurotypes.

Context Awareness: Improve models by considering situational context instead of relying solely on facial expressions.

Transparent Algorithms: Companies should disclose how models are trained and tested to prevent hidden biases

Human Oversight: Emotion recognition systems should not operate autonomously in high-stakes decisions (e.g., law enforcement, hiring).

Regulations & Ethical AI: Governments should enforce AI ethics guidelines to prevent misuse of biased emotion recognition in surveillance

Allow Opt-Out in Workplaces and Schools: Neurodivergent individuals should have the right to disable emotion AI tracking that may misinterpret them.

Include Neurodivergent Voices in AI Development: Involve neurodivergent individuals in testing and designing AI systems.
